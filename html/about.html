About Me.

Hi, my name is Christoper James Lebioda.

I'm a part-time Graduate student at the University of Michigan studying User Experience Design/User-Centered Agile Design at the University of Michigan within the School of Information. I previously got my undergraduate degree at the U of M in Computer Science and Economics (check out the timeline on the home page for that journey). As a future designer/developer, I want to shrink the technology gap for the elderly and children with autism.


I should have known I wanted to work with computers in some form or another early in life.

Growing up, my dad brought home interesting technology because he worked on the entertainment systems at Ford. We had the luxery of being able to play Mario Kart on the N64 in the van because he needed real users to test the system they were creating. Now that I am writing this, that was my first experience with user design! Although the multiple systems he brought home through the years essentially worked the same, he was bridging the gap between how things function and how things looked.


As I left for college in 2013 and my dad slowly turned to the managing side of engineering, I noticed my dad still loves technology (playing with Arduinos, figuring out how Chromecast works, etc.), but the cost of entry is much greater than before. As technology advances and becomes integrated in or even replaces the most basic aspects of our daily lives, we need to ensure the technology itself is accessible by all members of our society. Although more and more technologies claim to be accessible and inclusive, they often alienate and overwhelm older populations because most products inherently assume their users have used something similar in the past. Even if we have the most sophisticated, robust, and well-implemented piece of technology, if users cannot understand how it works with relative ease, then the design is a failure. Especially for older adults, barriers like lack of instructions and guidance, complexity, and feelings of inadequacy drive them away from even the most “accessible” technology. I believe if we better understand the perceptions of an older generation, then we can design interfaces for them and close the technology age gap.
